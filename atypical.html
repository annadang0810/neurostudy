Big Picture

"Atypical" neurotransmitters break the classical rules: they aren't stored in vesicles by VMAT, they often aren't released by exocytosis, and they can travel backward across a synapse.

  • Endocannabinoids — lipids made on demand, retrograde messengers (CB1/CB2)
  • Purines — ATP, ADP, adenosine; act on P2X (channels), P2Y & P1 (GPCRs)
  • Nitric oxide — gas, freely diffuses, activates guanylate cyclase

Cannabinoid Receptors

Both CB1 and CB2 are G-protein–coupled receptors (typically Gi/o).

CB1

  • Highly expressed in brain — especially cerebral cortex, prefrontal cortex, basal ganglia, hippocampus, amygdala, hypothalamus, cerebellum, periaqueductal gray, NTS, brainstem, spinal cord
  • Also: adipose tissue, muscle, liver, GI tract, pancreas, reproductive & CV tissues

CB2

  • Primarily on immune cells: T cells, B cells, monocytes, spleen, tonsils
  • Some neuronal expression is being studied

CB1 ligands — 3 types

  • Endogenous (endocannabinoids): AEA (anandamide), 2-AG (2-arachidonoylglycerol)
  • Plant-derived: Δ⁹-tetrahydrocannabinol (Δ⁹-THC), cannabidiol
  • Synthetic agonists/antagonists (e.g., rimonabant, URB597)

Endocannabinoid rules

  • Made from membrane phospholipids
  • NOT stored in vesicles — synthesized and immediately released
  • In brain: retrograde messengers; elsewhere: autocrine / paracrine
  • Subsequently degraded by FAAH (AEA) or MGL/MAGL (2-AG)

Pharmacological tools

  • URB597 — FAAH inhibitor (raises AEA)
  • AM404 — endocannabinoid membrane transporter inhibitor
  • Rimonabant — CB1 antagonist (Europe-approved for obesity, later withdrawn for psychiatric side effects)

Retrograde signaling

Postsynaptic Ca²⁺ rise and/or mGluR/mAChR activation → lipid precursor → endocannabinoid → diffuses backward to presynaptic CB1 (Gi) → ↓ Ca²⁺ entry → ↓ neurotransmitter release.

CNS-Regulated Processes & Therapeutic Apps

Feeding & energy balance

  • CB1 in hypothalamus
  • Endocannabinoids stimulate food intake in a CB1-dependent manner
  • CB1 antagonists (rimonabant, taranabant, CP-945,598) → weight loss via ↓ intake + ↑ energy use; rimonabant approved in Europe for obesity

Emesis (nausea / vomiting)

  • Cannabinoid agonists suppress nausea and vomiting
  • Endogenous CB1 "tone" may inhibit anticipatory nausea

Pain management

  • Selective CB1 agonists are analgesic
  • Oromucosal spray: Δ⁹-THC + cannabidiol — approved in Canada for MS-related neuropathic pain & advanced cancer pain
  • CB2 also inhibits pain perception (anti-nociception); role in chronic inflammatory & neuropathic pain
  • Novel strategy: endocannabinoid-degradation inhibitors (FAAH/MGL) + selective CB2 agonists

Purinergic Signaling

Signaling molecules

Nucleotide / nucleoside derivatives of adenine, guanine, uracil, thymidine, cytosine. Most common: ATP, ADP, AMP, cAMP, adenosine; also UTP, UDP.

Structure: base + ribose = nucleoside (e.g., adenosine); add phosphate(s) = nucleotide (e.g., adenosine 5′-monophosphate).

Breakdown (extracellular, <1 second)

ATP → ADP → AMP by ectodiphosphohydrolase
AMP → adenosine by ectonucleotidase / ecto-5′-nucleotidase

Storage & release

  • Loaded into vesicles by VNUT (vesicular nucleotide transporter)
  • Often co-released with classic neurotransmitters from the same vesicle
  • Also released via: ATP transporters, connexin/pannexin hemichannels, voltage-dependent anion channels, P2X7 channel (large pore)

Key distributions

  • A1: brain, spinal cord, heart — antagonists ↑ catecholamines
  • A2A: brain, heart, lungs, spleen
  • P2X3: sensory neurons, NTS — ATP-mediated taste, carotid body, bladder afferents
  • P2X4 on microglia — activation in dorsal horn after nerve injury triggers BDNF release → neuropathic pain
  • P2Y12: platelets & glia — target of antiplatelet drugs (clopidogrel)

Where it shows up

  • Neuron-to-neuron throughout CNS & PNS
  • Neuron-to-glia (astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, Schwann cells, microglia)
  • Glia-to-glia (ATP is the dominant signal)
  • Pathology: excessive ATP from injury/overstimulation → reactive astrocytes & activated microglia → pro-inflammatory cascades — implicated in Alzheimer's and chronic pain

Caffeine & Methylxanthines

  • Caffeine = adenosine receptor antagonist
  • Adenosine normally inhibits catecholamine release at A2A; blocking A2A → ↑ catecholamines (alertness, ↑ HR, ↑ BP)

Nitric Oxide (NO) Signaling

  • Originally identified as the endothelial-derived relaxation factor
  • A gaseous signaling molecule — freely diffuses across membranes; not packaged or transported

Synthesis

L-arginine + O₂ → NO + citrulline, by nitric oxide synthase (NOS), activated by Ca²⁺ – calmodulin.

IsoformWhereNotes
nNOS (neuronal)Exclusively neuronsCoupled to NMDA via PSD-95
eNOS (endothelial)Endothelial cells + neuronsVasodilation
iNOS (inducible)Immune cells + neuronsInduced by inflammation; Ca²⁺-independent

Targets & effects

  • Activates soluble guanylate cyclase (sGC) → GTP → cGMP in neighboring cells
  • Can S-nitrosylate cysteine residues on proteins (post-translational modification)
  • Reacts with O₂ radicals (superoxide) → peroxynitrite (ONOO⁻) → damages neurons in ischemia–reperfusion (stroke)

Retrograde signaling at glutamate synapses

Glutamate → postsynaptic NMDA receptors → Ca²⁺ entry → activates nNOS (with CaM, scaffolded by PSD-95) → NO diffuses backward → presynaptic sGC → cGMP → modulates release.

Common themes

  • Many serve as retrograde messengers — postsynaptic activity feeds back to tune presynaptic release
  • They tend to be made on demand, not stockpiled
  • They often modulate other transmitters rather than carry the primary signal
