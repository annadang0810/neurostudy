Big Picture

Two opposing classes — one drives the brain up, the other shuts it down — but both work through the same kinds of neurotransmitter and channel systems.

  • Caffeine — adenosine receptor antagonist → disinhibition → ↑ DA, glutamate, epinephrine
  • Cocaine — blocks monoamine reuptake (NE, 5-HT, DA)
  • Ethanol — ↑ GABA-A, ↓ NMDA, ↓ Ca²⁺ channels, ↓ adenosine uptake; broad polypharmacology

Caffeine — America's #1 Drug

  • ~90% of Americans consume caffeine daily; over half consume >300 mg/day
  • Sources: chocolate, coffee, tea, diet pills, OTC pain relievers
  • Medical: cardiac stimulant, mild diuretic. Recreational: alertness, "boost of energy"
  • Addictive — uses the same dopamine reward circuits as amphetamines, cocaine, heroin (but milder)

Intoxication and overdose

  • >250 mg in a short time → restlessness, insomnia, muscle twitching, GI upset, cardiac arrhythmia
  • Lethal dose: 5–10 g (≈ 30–60 cups of coffee in one morning)

How caffeine produces its effects

  1. Blocks adenosine A1 receptors → disinhibition → ↑ neuronal firing, ↑ release of dopamine and glutamate
  2. Blocks A2A in basal ganglia → ↑ motor activity (dopamine-like)
  3. Constricts cerebral blood vessels (blocks adenosine vasodilation) — useful in vascular headache meds
  4. Increased neuronal firing prompts pituitary → adrenal release of epinephrine → "fight or flight"

Epinephrine ("fight or flight") effects

  • Pupils dilate; air passages open
  • ↑ HR, ↑ BP; surface vessels constrict (cold hands)
  • ↓ blood flow to stomach; liver releases sugar; muscles tense

Pharmacokinetics & the cycle

  • Half-life ≈ 6 hours: 200 mg at 3 pm → 100 mg still onboard at 9 pm → loss of deep sleep
  • Withdrawal: fatigue, depression, splitting headache (rebound cerebral vasodilation)

Adenosine in the Brain

  • Adenosine is found everywhere (ATP metabolism), but in the brain it acts as an inhibitory neurotransmitter
  • Levels in the basal forebrain rise with each hour of wakefulness → drowsiness
  • Adenosine also dilates blood vessels (likely ↑ O₂ supply during sleep)

Caffeine is a non-selective adenosine receptor antagonist — mostly A1 and A2A account for its CNS effects.

Ethanol — Mechanisms of Action

Despite long study, ethanol's mechanism is among the least understood of all psychoactive drugs — it affects virtually every neurochemical and endocrine system. Four major theories:

  1. Enhances GABA-A receptor signaling → ↑ Cl⁻ influx → hyperpolarization (similar to BZDs but smaller and less consistent)
  2. Inhibits voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels → ↓ presynaptic neurotransmitter release
  3. Inhibits NMDA receptors → blunts glutamate excitation (NMDA is more sensitive than AMPA at lower ethanol concentrations)
  4. Inhibits the adenosine transporter → ↑ extracellular adenosine → A1-mediated CNS depression

Other receptor effects

  • Enhances nicotinic ACh and 5-HT₃ excitation
  • Recruits endogenous opioids — naltrexone reduces ethanol's reward, supporting an opioid component to alcohol's pleasure

Forms of alcohol you may encounter: ethanol (drinkable), methanol (toxic, antifreeze ingredient), ethylene glycol (antifreeze) — all CNS depressants but methanol & ethylene glycol are systemically toxic.

Ethanol — CNS Effects & BAC

1 standard US drink = 14 g ethanol (0.3 mol) → raises BAC ~0.02–0.05% (20–50 mg/100 mL).

Driving

  • BAC 0.05%: crash probability unaffected
  • BAC 0.08%: 4× crash risk — legal limit in US/UK
  • BAC 0.15%: 25× crash risk

Subjects are generally unable to judge their own impairment.

Ethanol — Liver, Fetus, Metabolism

Hepatic metabolism

  • ~75% via alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH): ethanol → acetaldehyde
  • ~25% via mixed-function oxidase (CYP2E1, MEOS): ethanol → acetaldehyde
  • Acetaldehyde → acetate via aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH) (~75%) or aldehyde oxidase (~25%)
  • Disulfiram blocks ALDH → acetaldehyde accumulates → flushing, nausea, vomiting → aversion therapy for alcoholism

Liver damage progression

Fatty liver → alcoholic hepatitis → irreversible necrosis & fibrosis (cirrhosis).

Chronic alcoholics may meet caloric needs from ethanol alone (300 g ≈ 2000 kcal) but with no vitamins, amino acids, or fatty acids — malnutrition contributes, but cellular toxicity of ethanol itself is the main driver.

Tolerance & Withdrawal (Ethanol)

Tolerance

  • Develops over 1–3 weeks; ~2–3-fold reduction in potency
  • Small contribution from faster elimination; main mechanism is tissue tolerance (not fully understood)
  • Cross-tolerance to many anesthetics — alcoholics are harder to anesthetize with agents like halothane

Withdrawal syndrome

A well-defined physical abstinence syndrome can develop in severe form within ~8 hours of last drink. Subsides over a few days, but craving and relapse risk last much longer.

StageOnsetSymptoms
1st stage~24 hTremor, nausea, sweating, fever, hallucinations, possible seizures ('rum fits')
2nd stage — delirium tremensDays 2–4Confusion, agitation, aggression, severe hallucinations — potentially life-threatening

Why benzodiazepines treat alcohol withdrawal

BZDs and ethanol both potentiate GABA-A. Substituting a longer-acting BZD (chlordiazepoxide, diazepam) prevents GABA collapse and the rebound glutamatergic excitation responsible for seizures and DTs — allowing controlled tapering.
