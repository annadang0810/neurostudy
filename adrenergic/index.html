Norepinephrine in the Nervous System

  • Norepinephrine (NE) is produced by neurons in the pons and medulla
  • The locus ceruleus (LC) in the pons contains >50% of NE neurons in the brain and provides almost all NE innervation to the cortex
  • NE signaling contributes to the sleep-wake cycle (arousal), attention, and anxiety
  • NE-modulating drugs treat ADHD: atomoxetine (Strattera), desipramine (Norpramin)
  • NE acting on β-receptors in the amygdala increases memory for negative emotion (relevant to PTSD)

Catecholamine Biosynthesis Pathway

Pathway: Tyrosine → L-DOPA → Dopamine → NE → Epinephrine

  • Tyrosine hydroxylase (TH) is the rate-limiting enzyme
  • TH is thought to be near saturated under normal conditions
  • TH is inhibited by catecholamines — feedback inhibition
  • Tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4) is a required cofactor for TH (and tryptophan hydroxylase)

Catecholamine Degradation

  • Catecholamines are degraded by monoamine oxidase (MAO) or catechol-O-methyltransferase (COMT)

MAO Isoforms

  • MAOA mostly in noradrenergic neurons (including dopaminergic neurons) — metabolizes 5HT, NE, EPI, DA
  • MAOB mostly in serotonergic and histaminergic neurons — metabolizes DA

MAO Inhibitors (MAOIs) — Antidepressants

  • Isocarboxazid, phenelzine, tranylcypromine, selegiline
  • Inhibit breakdown of catecholamines, increasing their synaptic availability

Degradation Products

  • DA → Homovanillic acid (HVA)
  • NE → Vanillylmandelic acid (VMA) and 3-methoxy-4-hydroxyphenylglycol (MHPG)

Catecholamine Regulation

  • Diffusion away from synapses
  • Metabolic transformation (MAO, COMT)
  • Re-uptake into nerve terminals via catecholamine transporters

Catecholamine Transporters

  • Selective, pharmacologically distinct transport proteins that remove NE, DA, and 5HT from extracellular space
  • NET Norepinephrine Transporter
  • DAT Dopamine Transporter
  • SERT Serotonin Transporter

Structure

  • 12 transmembrane domain proteins
  • Intracellular carboxyl and amino tails
  • Large extracellular loop between TMDs III and IV with multiple N-glycosylation sites
  • Potential phosphorylation sites on intracellular domains — evidence of kinase-mediated regulation

Vesicular Monoamine Transporter (VMAT)

  • VMAT pumps NE into vesicles (also DA and 5HT)
  • 2 isoforms: VMAT1 and VMAT2
  • Inhibited by reserpine and tetrabenazine
  • Guanethidine is taken up by NET into noradrenergic neurons, concentrated in vesicles, eventually depletes NE from vesicles. Does not cross the BBB

Transporter Pharmacology — Stimulants

Cocaine

  • Inhibits re-uptake of NE, Epi, and DA by blocking NET/DAT

Methylphenidate (Ritalin)

  • Blocks NET/DAT
  • Probably does not lead to increased NE/DA release

Amphetamine

  • Inhibits re-uptake of NE/DA via competition — amphetamine is a substrate for NET/DAT
  • Depletes NE/DA from vesicles via disruption of proton gradient
  • Stimulates efflux of NE/DA through NET/DAT via phosphorylation
  • Weak MAO inhibitor

Autoreceptors (α2 Presynaptic)

  • Autoreceptors for NE are on presynaptic terminals
  • Release of NE binds to presynaptic α2 receptors
  • This leads to a decrease in TH activity and decrease in NE release
  • This is a negative feedback mechanism regulating noradrenergic transmission

α1-Adrenergic Pharmacology

Agonists

  • Phenylephrine — nasal decongestant
  • Methoxamine, oxymetazoline

Antagonists

  • Prazosin — anti-hypertensive
  • Terazosin, tamsulosin

α2-Adrenergic Pharmacology

Agonists

  • Clonidine (Catapres) — antihypertensive, ADHD, craving symptoms

Antagonists

  • Yohimbine (Procomil)
  • Mirtazapine (Remeron) — antidepressant

β-Adrenergic Pharmacology (General)

Agonist

  • Isoproterenol — emergency bradycardia, cardiac shock

Antagonist

  • Propranolol — migraine prophylaxis, blood pressure, performance anxiety

β1-Adrenergic Pharmacology

Agonist

  • Dobutamine — cardiogenic shock

Antagonist

  • Atenolol (drugs ending in -olol) — angina, recurrent heart attacks

β2-Adrenergic Pharmacology

Agonist

  • Albuterol — asthma

Antagonist

  • Butoxamine — characterization and identification of β2 activity in research

Receptor Desensitization

Loss of response of receptor in the continued presence of ligand.

Three General Mechanisms

  • Receptor phosphorylation — by PKC, β-ARK (β-adrenergic receptor kinase), G-protein coupled receptor kinases (GRKs)
  • Receptor internalization/sequestration — phosphorylated receptors are bound by β-arrestin, marking them for internalization
  • Receptor downregulation — via either internalization or degradation
