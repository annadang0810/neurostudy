Big Picture

Anxiety = an unpleasant state of tension, apprehension, or uneasiness — a fear without a real or logical cause. Treat by class:

  • Benzodiazepines — acute anxiety; potentiate GABA-A; tolerance + dependence
  • SSRIs / SNRIs — first-line for panic, OCD, GAD long-term
  • Buspirone — 5-HT1A agonist; chronic anxiety, no sedation, slow onset
  • β-blockers — only the somatic symptoms (tremor, palpitations)
  • Flumazenil — competitive BZD antagonist (rescue)

The Nature of Anxiety

Persistent memories of intense fear can establish maladaptive associative emotional memories → pathological anxiety. Unlike fear, in anxiety there is no immediately threatening stimulus.

Definition

Anxiety = an unpleasant state of tension, apprehension, or uneasiness — a fear that seems to arise without a real or logical cause.

Chief manifestations

  • Somatic / autonomic effects: restlessness, agitation, tachycardia, sweating, weeping, GI disorders, sleep disturbances
  • Interference with normal productive activities

Epidemiology & clinical context

  • Episodes of mild anxiety are common life experiences and don't warrant treatment
  • Anxiolytic drugs were used regularly by >10% of the population in many developed countries
  • They've fallen out of favor as uncertain benefit + definite risks have become evident
  • Same drugs treat insomnia — sedation/drowsiness is a main drawback
  • In high doses, all anxiolytics cause unconsciousness and eventually death from respiratory and cardiovascular depression

Classification of Anxiolytic / Hypnotic Drugs

  • Benzodiazepines — the most important class; treat both anxiety and insomnia
  • 5-HT1A receptor agonists (e.g., buspirone) — anxiolytic with little sedation
  • Barbiturates — largely obsolete as anxiolytics; dangerous overdose, dependence
  • β-adrenoceptor antagonists — reduce physical symptoms (tremor, palpitations); no effect on the affective component

Benzodiazepines: Mechanism of Action

  • First BZD: chlordiazepoxide (Librium), synthesized by accident in 1961 at Hoffman-La Roche
  • ~20 BZDs in clinical use; pharmacologically similar but with selectivity differences (e.g., clonazepam — anti-convulsant, less sedation)

Where they act

  • Selective for the GABA-A receptor (a Cl⁻-permeable pentameric ligand-gated channel: 2α + 2β + γ)
  • BZDs do NOT affect glycine or glutamate receptors

How they work

BZDs bind a distinct allosteric site (BZD site, on the α/γ interface) and enhance GABA's effect — they increase the channel's affinity for GABA and the frequency of Cl⁻ channel opening (vs. barbiturates, which prolong opening duration).

Benzodiazepines: Effects, Risks, & Dependence

Five main pharmacological effects

  • Reduction of anxiety + aggression ("taming" effect in animals)
  • Sedation and induction of sleep
  • Reduction of muscle tone + impaired coordination
  • Anticonvulsant activity
  • Anterograde amnesia

No antidepressant action — with the possible exception of alprazolam.

Acute toxicity

BZDs in acute overdose are considerably less dangerous than other anxiolytic/hypnotic drugs — cause prolonged sleep without serious respiratory or cardiovascular depression (unlike barbiturates).

Side effects in therapeutic use

  • Drowsiness, confusion, amnesia, impaired coordination → affects manual skills (e.g., driving)

Tolerance & dependence (the main drawback)

  • All BZDs produce tolerance (appears to be a receptor-level change — mechanism unclear) and dependence
  • Stopping after weeks/months: ↑ anxiety, tremor, dizziness
  • Withdrawal after chronic use resembles opioid withdrawal: nervousness, tremor, loss of appetite, sometimes convulsions

Shared profile

  • MOA Bind BZD (ω) site on GABA-A → ↑ affinity for GABA, ↑ frequency of Cl⁻ channel opening → potentiate inhibition
  • USE Anxiety, status epilepticus, muscle spasm, alcohol withdrawal, preoperative sedation, light anesthesia, anterograde amnesia for procedures
  • PK Oral / IV / IM; hepatic metabolism, urinary excretion; many have active metabolites (long-acting)
  • AE Dizziness, drowsiness, lethargy, physical + psychological dependence, tolerance
  • DDI Additive CNS depression with alcohol and other CNS depressants

SSRIs / SNRIs

  • Selectively block 5-HT (and NE for SNRIs) reuptake transporter
  • Greater efficacy and lower side-effect burden vs benzos/barbiturates for chronic use
  • Can transiently worsen anxiety before anxiolytic effect appears (~weeks)
  • Side effects: agitation, nausea, diarrhea, ↓ libido, anorgasmia, weight gain; SNRIs add HTN & tachycardia
  • SSRI-Discontinuation Syndrome on stopping/decreasing dose

Buspirone (BuSpar)

  • Agonist at the 5-HT1A receptor
  • Relieves anxiety without sedation
  • Slow onset (~1 week); for chronic anxiety (GAD)
  • Low addiction potential (like SSRIs)
  • No muscle relaxation, no anticonvulsant activity
  • Not as effective as benzos or SSRIs
  • Vilazodone (Viibryd) = SSRI + 5-HT1A agonist for depression
