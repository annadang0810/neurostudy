Big Picture

Three concepts to keep separate:

  • Tolerance — the body becomes less responsive to a dose; user must take more to feel the same effect.
  • Dependence — a stable change in brain function; stopping causes a withdrawal syndrome. Even non-addictive drugs (SSRIs, antipsychotics) can cause dependence.
  • Addiction — compulsive drug-seeking behavior, relapse, and dulled response to natural rewards. Implies physical AND psychological dependence.

Virtually all addictive drugs converge on the mesolimbic dopamine reward pathway (VTA → NAc) regardless of their primary molecular target.

Key Definitions

TermMeaning
Drug abuseAny recurrent use of substances that are illegal or that cause harm to the individual.
Drug dependenceBody's physical need for a specific agent — a stable change in brain function. Stopping → withdrawal symptoms. Implies physical and/or psychological dependence.
Drug addictionCompulsive drug taking that takes precedence over other needs; drug-seeking behavior, vulnerability to relapse, and decreased response to natural rewards.
ToleranceBody becomes less responsive to a given dose → must increase intake to recapture the same effect. A defining feature of dependence.
Withdrawal / abstinence syndromePhysical AND psychological adverse effects after stopping a drug; lasts days to weeks; intensity and nature vary by drug class.

Dependence ≠ addiction

Several psychotropic drugs (antidepressants, antipsychotics) produce withdrawal symptoms but are not addictive. So the two terms must be distinguished.

Reward, Reinforcement & Conditioning

"Drugs of abuse" are pharmacologically heterogeneous but share one feature: their effect is hedonic (pleasurable) — users want to repeat it.

Mesolimbic dopamine pathway (the "reward pathway")

  • Begins in the ventral tegmental area (VTA) in the midbrain
  • Projects to: nucleus accumbens, amygdala, hippocampus, prefrontal cortex
  • Mediates motivation, reward, and behavior — makes survival behaviors feel good
  • Virtually all dependence-producing drugs activate this pathway even though their primary molecular sites differ

Two kinds of reinforcement

TypeMechanism
Positive reinforcementReward pathway is stimulated → user feels pleasure → repeats behavior
Negative reinforcementUser self-administers to escape aversive withdrawal symptoms

Conditioning (cues, contexts, paraphernalia) further sustains dependence: drug-associated cues evoke craving; cues associated with cessation become aversive.

In long-term dependence, psychological reinforcement — not physical dependence — is the main driver of relapse.

Warning signs of drug abuse (selected)

  • Secrecy / withdrawal; depressed or suicidal
  • Loss of focus on customary activities; trouble with the law
  • Cycles of energy / restlessness / inability to sleep or abnormally slow movements / speech
  • Confusion or disorientation; sudden weight loss or gain

Controlled Substances Act (CSA, 1970s)

  • Regulates manufacture, importation, possession, and distribution of certain drugs
  • Created 5 Schedules; oversight by Department of Justice (DEA) and Department of Health & Human Services
  • Explicitly exempt: tobacco, beer, wine, spirits, caffeine

Schedule I criteria (all three must apply)

  1. High potential for abuse
  2. No currently accepted medical use in US treatment
  3. Lack of accepted safety for use under medical supervision

No prescriptions may be written for Schedule I; production is quota-limited by the DEA.

Pharmacological Approaches to Treating Dependence

StrategyExamples
Substitution to alleviate withdrawalMethadone short-term for opiate withdrawal · Benzodiazepines for alcohol withdrawal · Clonidine (α₂ agonist) to diminish withdrawal symptoms
Long-term substitutionMethadone maintenance for opioid use disorder · Nicotine patches / gum for smoking cessation
Blocking therapiesNaltrexone blocks opioid effects (and reduces alcohol reward) · Mecamylamine blocks nicotine effects · immunization against cocaine / nicotine (investigational)

Why "substitution" works

Replacing a short-acting, high-reinforcement drug (heroin, alcohol, short-acting BZD) with a longer-acting, less-rewarding agonist at the same target prevents withdrawal — while removing the rapid "high" that drives compulsive use. The patient can then taper.

Stimulants — Amphetamine / Methamphetamine

  • Produce alertness, increased energy, euphoria
  • Routes and onset:
    • Smoking / IV — immediate intense rush, lasts only minutes
    • Snorting — euphoria after 3–5 min (no intense rush)
    • Swallowing — euphoria after 15–20 min (no intense rush)

"Binge abuse cycle"

StageDurationFeatures
Rush5–30 min↑ HR, BP, metabolism; intense pleasure
High4–16 hAggressive, "smarter," argumentative
Binge3–15 daysHyperactive mentally and physically; sustains the high
Tweaking(Most dangerous stage)No sleep 3–15 days; irritable, paranoid, violent; eyes move ~10× faster than normal; voice quiver; quick jerky movements; no dose recreates euphoria
Crash → normal → withdrawalUp to 30–90 daysDepression, lethargy, suicidal craving, fatigue, excessive sleep, agitation, possible psychosis

Chronic features: extreme weight loss, pale skin, body odor, discolored / rotten teeth ("meth mouth"), open sores from "crank bug" hallucinations of bugs on skin.

Treatment: motivation; avoid all stimulants (incl. caffeine and tobacco); behavioral programs; antidepressants (depression/craving), sedatives (anxiety/sleep), antipsychotics (paranoia/cravings).

Stimulants — Cocaine

RouteOnset of intense rush
IV (injection)15–30 sec
Smoking (freebase / crack)5–8 sec
Snorting (intranasal)3–5 min to brain (self-limiting; vasoconstriction slows further absorption)

Forms

  • Cocaine hydrochloride — water-soluble: drinking, snorting, injecting
  • Freebase cocaine — water-insoluble: smoking
  • Crack — freebase made with sodium bicarbonate; smokeable

Withdrawal

Anhedonia, anergia, loss of motivation, emotional depression, nightmares, insomnia, hunger.

Overdose ("caine reaction")

Triggered by anywhere from 1/50 g to 1.2 g. Most users survive the cold-clammy sweaty sensation with a feeling of impending death. Fatalities, however, do occur from cardiac arrest, seizures, respiratory failure, and severe hyperthermia.

Treatment: no specific medication — behavioral programs and treatment programs.

Depressants — Benzodiazepines

  • Examples: diazepam, clonazepam, lorazepam
  • Abused for sedative + anxiolytic effects
  • Withdrawal: similar to alcohol withdrawal (anxiety, tremor, hallucinations, possible seizures)
  • Treatment: transfer the dependent patient to an equivalent dose of diazepam, then gradual taper

Depressants — Barbiturates

  • Examples: phenobarbital, pentobarbital, amobarbital
  • Intoxication resembles ethanol — relaxed contentment, euphoria
  • Main risk of acute abuse: respiratory depression (much narrower therapeutic window than BZDs)

Withdrawal (12–20 h after last dose)

  • Anxiety, irritability, ↑ HR & RR, muscle pain, nausea, tremors, hallucinations, confusion, seizures
  • Barbiturates ↓ REM sleep → withdrawal often produces nightmares, insomnia, vivid dreaming (REM rebound)

Treatment: transfer to equivalent diazepam, then taper.

Depressants — Methaqualone (Quaaludes)

  • Sedative similar in effect to barbiturates; popular as a hypnotic / sedative / muscle relaxant in the 1960s–70s
  • Actions: relaxation, euphoria, drowsiness, ↓ HR/RR, ↑ sexual arousal, numbness of fingers/toes; high doses → depression, ataxia, slurred speech, headache, photophobia
  • Overdose: delirium, convulsions, hypertonia, hyperreflexia, vomiting, renal insufficiency, coma, cardiac/respiratory arrest
  • Treatment: diazepam substitution and taper

Cannabinoids

Marijuana = dried leaves and flower heads. Hashish = extracted resin. Cannabis extracts contain >60 lipid compounds called cannabinoids.

Receptors

  • CB1 — the most abundant GPCR in the human brain. High in hippocampus, cortex, basal ganglia, cerebellum, spinal cord → explains effects on memory, cognition, movement.
  • CB2 — immune cells and other peripheral tissues

Cannabinoid receptor signaling (Gi/o)

  • Directly inhibit N- and P/Q-type Ca²⁺ channels
  • Indirectly inhibit A-type Ca²⁺ channels via adenylyl cyclase
  • Directly inhibit Na⁺ channels
  • Activate inwardly rectifying K⁺ channels
  • Activate the MAP-kinase signaling pathway

Δ⁹-THC

AspectDetail
MechanismΔ⁹-tetrahydrocannabinol — agonist at CB1 + CB2
ActionsEuphoria, drowsiness, relaxation; impairs short-term memory and skilled motor activity (e.g., driving); catalepsy; analgesia; antiemetic; ↑ appetite; xerostomia; high doses: hallucinations, delusions, toxic psychosis
Adverse↑ HR, ↓ BP, conjunctival redness, ↓ intraocular pressure, bronchodilation; tolerance and mild physical dependence

Pharmacokinetics

  • Highly lipophilic — sequestered into body fat; large volume of distribution
  • Peak effects ~20 min after smoking; gone after ~3 h. Dronabinol oral peak 2–4 h; psychoactivity ~6 h; appetite stimulation up to 24 h
  • Metabolized by mixed-function oxidases; small amount → 11-hydroxy-THC (more active than Δ⁹-THC); enterohepatic recirculation; biliary elimination — can persist 3 months in body

Clinical uses

DrugUse
Dronabinol (Marinol)Appetite stimulant in AIDS
Nabilone (Cesamet)Synthetic cannabinoid — antiemetic, neuropathic pain, AIDS appetite
SativexOromucosal Δ⁹-THC + CBD — neuropathic pain & MS spasticity
RimonabantCB1 antagonist (anti-obesity, antiemetic in chemo) — withdrawn for psychiatric side effects

Withdrawal (often missed)

Because cannabinoids leach slowly out of fat, immediate withdrawal is mild — leading some to think it doesn't occur. Symptoms: irritability/anger, depression, sleep disturbance, ↓ appetite, sweating, tremors, craving. Tolerance develops rapidly.
