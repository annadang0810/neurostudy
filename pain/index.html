Big Picture

Pain ≠ nociception. Nociception is the neural encoding; pain is the conscious experience (sensory + affective). Therapeutic goal: eliminate abnormal pain without interfering with normal protective pain.

  • Opioids — act at μ/δ/κ GPCRs to ↓ presynaptic Ca²⁺ + ↑ postsynaptic K⁺ → analgesia (and respiratory depression, addiction)
  • NSAIDs / acetaminophen — inhibit COX → ↓ prostaglandins → less peripheral nociceptor sensitization
  • Local anesthetics — block voltage-gated Na⁺ channels → halt action potentials in nociceptors

What is Pain?

IASP definition: "an unpleasant sensory and emotional experience associated with actual or potential tissue damage, or described in terms of such damage."

Nociception: the neural process of encoding noxious stimuli (≠ the conscious feeling of pain).

Four neural steps in pain processing

  • Transduction — noxious stimuli are converted to electrical signals at sensory nerve endings
  • Transmission — relays the signal from periphery to cortex
  • Modulation — the nervous system selectively inhibits transmission
  • Perception — subjective interpretation by cortex; has a sensory component AND an affective component

Acute vs chronic pain

Acute (normal, protective)Chronic (abnormal, "pain as disease")
FunctionReflexes; signals injury; drives inflammation/repairNon-protective; persists past healing
ExamplesBurns, post-op, sprainsLow back pain, fibromyalgia, neuropathy, PHN

Therapeutic goal: eliminate abnormal pain without interfering with normal, protective pain.

The brain is necessary for pain. Activation of nociceptors signals to many regions: spinal cord → thalamus → S1/S2, insula, ACC, prefrontal cortex; also amygdala, hippocampus, basal ganglia, brainstem. Pain is influenced by mood (depression, anxiety, catastrophizing), context (beliefs, expectation, placebo), and cognitive set (attention, distraction, hypervigilance).

Ascending and Descending Pathways

  • Ascending pain system — transmits info from nerve endings to brain via spinothalamic / spinoparabrachial / spinoreticular tracts
  • Descending pain system — brain modulates incoming signals via projections to spinal cord (PAG → RVM/LC → dorsal horn)

DRG & dorsal horn

  • Dorsal root ganglion (DRG): thousands of cell bodies — touch, temperature, pain, itch — one DRG on each side at each spinal level
  • Primary afferents synapse in the dorsal horn (Rexed laminae I–V/VI for nociception)
  • From there, two parallel ascending streams:
    • Lateral spinothalamic tract → lateral thalamus → somatosensory cortex (sensory-discriminative aspect)
    • Spinoparabrachial tract → limbic centers (emotional/aversive aspect)

Central regions activated by nociception

Spinal cord, thalamus, S1/S2, insula, anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), prefrontal cortex; amygdala, hippocampus, posterior parietal cortex, basal ganglia, brainstem, PAG.

Sensitization & Inflammation

  • Allodynia — perception of pain from a normally non-painful stimulus (e.g., light touch hurts)
  • Hyperalgesia — exaggerated pain from a normally painful stimulus

After injury, the curve of pain intensity vs stimulus temperature shifts left (allodynia) and up (hyperalgesia).

Inflammation sensitizes nociceptors via prostaglandins

  • Phospholipase A₂ liberates arachidonic acid from membranes
  • COX-1 (constitutive) and COX-2 (inducible in inflammation) produce prostaglandins, thromboxanes, prostacyclins
  • Prostaglandins act on EP/IP receptors → Gs/Gq → ↑ cAMP/PKA & ↑ PKCε → phosphorylate & sensitize TRPV1, TRPA1, voltage-gated Na⁺ channels and inhibit K⁺ channels (HCN2, TREK, CAKC)
  • Net effect: nociceptors fire to weaker stimuli → clinical hyperalgesia
  • This is why NSAIDs (COX inhibitors) reduce inflammatory pain

Opioids

Plant alkaloids (esp. morphine) from opium poppy sap are the active ingredients. Morphine is acetylated to make heroin. Morphine, heroin, and synthetic opiates (methadone, fentanyl) are potent analgesics.

Three endogenous opioid peptide families

  • Endorphins ("endogenous morphine") — bind μ
  • Enkephalins — bind δ
  • Dynorphins — bind κ

Three opioid receptors (all GPCRs, mostly Gi/o)

ReceptorMain roleEndogenous ligand
μ (mu)Major analgesic target; also responsible for addiction, respiratory depression, euphoria, miosis, constipationEndorphins
δ (delta)Minor analgesic targetEnkephalins
κ (kappa)Possible pro-nociceptive; produces dysphoriaDynorphin

Mechanism of opioid analgesia

  • Presynaptic: block voltage-gated Ca²⁺ influx + open K⁺ channels (efflux) → ↓ excitatory neurotransmitter release (glutamate, substance P)
  • Postsynaptic: open K⁺ channels → hyperpolarize 2nd-order neuron → inhibit AP generation
  • Receptors located along the entire pain axis: peripheral C/Aδ terminals, immune cells, dorsal horn (laminae I–V), PAG, RVM, LC (drives descending NE inhibition)

Opioid side effects

Emesis, constipation, somnolence, confusion, respiratory depression, abuse (and miosis, urinary retention, pruritus).

NSAIDs & Acetaminophen

NSAIDs = non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs — inhibit COX-1 and COX-2 → ↓ prostaglandins, thromboxanes, prostacyclins → ↓ inflammation, ↓ peripheral nociceptor sensitization, ↓ fever.

NSAID side effects

GI ulcers, kidney failure, liver failure, prolonged bleeding, nausea/vomiting, diarrhea/constipation, rash. Reye's syndrome: aspirin in children/teens with chickenpox or influenza.

Local Anesthetics

Block voltage-gated Na⁺ channels → halt action potential conduction in nociceptive fibers.

  • Lidocaine (Xylocaine) — workhorse
  • Bupivacaine — long-acting
  • Tetracaine, procaine, mepivacaine, benzocaine

Side effects: respiratory depression, cardiovascular problems, seizures (with systemic absorption / overdose).

Centrally-Acting Analgesics

  • Opioids — act at PAG, RVM, dorsal horn; effective against moderate-severe pain
  • Cannabinoids — analgesic profile similar to opioids (CB1 agonism)
  • α₂-adrenergic agonists (e.g., clonidine) — used as an "opioid holiday" during chronic opioid therapy; act on descending noradrenergic inhibition
  • NE reuptake inhibitors (TCAs, SNRIs) — ↑ NE in dorsal horn → activate α₂ inhibition
  • Selective ion-channel blockers — subtype-selective Ca²⁺ & Na⁺ channel antagonists modulate excitability with potentially fewer side effects

Descending pain modulation circuit

PAG → RVM & LC → dorsal horn. Opioids and cannabinoids work at PAG and RVM; α₂ agonists, opioids, TCAs, and SNRIs reinforce inhibition at the dorsal horn (NE acts on α₂ on primary afferent terminals and 2nd-order neurons).
