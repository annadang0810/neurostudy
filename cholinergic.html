Cholinergic Transmission at the Synapse

ACh Synthesis & Release

  • Choline + Acetyl CoA are combined by the enzyme choline acetyltransferase (ChAT) in the presynaptic terminal
  • ACh is loaded into vesicles by the vesicular ACh transporter (VAChT)
  • Upon depolarization, Ca2+ influx triggers vesicle fusion and ACh release

ACh Breakdown

  • Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) breaks down ACh into choline and acetate in the synaptic cleft
  • Choline is recycled back into the presynaptic terminal via the choline transporter

Toxins at the Synapse

  • Botulinum toxin & tetanus toxin - prevent ACh vesicle release
  • Black widow spider toxin (alpha-latrotoxin) - stimulates ACh release (uncouples Ca2+ from the release process)

Transport Inhibitors

  • Vesamicol - blocks the vesicular ACh transporter (VAChT)
  • Hemicholinium - blocks the choline uptake transporter

Nicotinic Receptors (nAChRs)

  • Ligand-gated ion channels (ionotropic)
  • Allow Na+ and Ca2+ influx when activated
  • Found at: neuromuscular junction, autonomic ganglia, CNS
  • Curare & mecamylamine - nicotinic antagonists
  • Varenicline - partial agonist at nicotinic receptors (used for smoking cessation)

Muscarinic Receptors (mAChRs)

  • G-protein coupled receptors (GPCRs) - 5 subtypes (M1-M5)
  • M1, M3, M5 couple to Gq/11 (excitatory, second messengers: IP3, DAG, Ca2+)
  • M2, M4 couple to Gi/o (inhibitory, decrease cAMP)
  • CNS locations: cortex, hippocampus, striatum, thalamus, basal forebrain, substantia nigra

M2 in the Heart

  • Activated by ACh from the vagus nerve
  • Leads to beta-gamma-dependent opening of GIRKs (G-protein inwardly rectifying K+ channels)
  • Effect: slows heart rate

Agonists & Antagonists

  • Agonists: carbachol, arecoline, oxotremorine, pilocarpine
  • Antagonists: atropine, scopolamine, benztropine

Anticholinergic Drugs (Muscarinic Antagonists)

  • Impact parasympathetic nervous system (outflow is cholinergic)
  • Overactive bladder: oxybutynin (Ditropan), tolterodine (Detrol), solifenacin (Vesicare)
  • Bronchodilators: ipratropium (Atrovent), tiotropium (Spiriva)
  • Pupillary dilation: cyclopentolate
  • Cholinesterase inhibitor overdose: atropine
  • Drugs with anticholinergic properties: first-gen antihistamines, tricyclic antidepressants, low-potency antipsychotics

Anticholinergic Side Effects (memorize!)

  • Dry mouth, dilated pupils, blurred vision
  • Decreased perspiration, elevated heart rate
  • Urinary retention, constipation
  • Dizziness, confusion, delirium

Sites of Cholinergic Neurotransmission: CNS

Basal Forebrain

  • Nucleus Basalis of Meynert - innervates sensory and limbic cortices
  • Involved in: arousal, emotion, cortical responses to sensory input
  • Also innervates learning/memory regions of cortex and hippocampus
  • Key for Alzheimer's - these neurons degenerate significantly in AD

Brainstem

  • Pedunculopontine nucleus and laterodorsal tegmental nucleus
  • Innervate: VTA, thalamus, cortex, basal ganglia, nucleus raphe magnus
  • Involved in: reward, sensory perception, movement

Striatum

  • Cholinergic interneurons - participate in motor learning and motor control

Other Cholinergic Neurons

  • Medial septum, cranial nerve nuclei, vestibular nuclei
  • Preganglionic and motor neurons
  • Habenula-interpeduncular pathway, thalamocortical/striatal pathways

Cholinesterase Inhibitors

Reversible

  • Physostigmine, neostigmine
  • Used for: glaucoma, myasthenia gravis, bladder dysfunction
  • Also includes carbamate insecticides

Irreversible

  • Organophosphates: parathion, malathion (pesticides)
  • Nerve gases: sarin, soman, tabun

Centrally Acting (for AD)

  • Tacrine (Cognex), donepezil (Aricept)
  • These cross the blood-brain barrier to act in the CNS

Myasthenia Gravis

  • Autoimmune channelopathy disorder
  • Immune system attacks nicotinic receptors at the neuromuscular junction
  • Auto-antibodies block AChRs, preventing muscle activation
  • Treated with: cholinesterase inhibitors or immunosuppressants

Alzheimer's Disease - Overview

  • Most common form of dementia (>50% of all dementias)
  • Affects 6-8% over age 65 and ~30% over age 85
  • Earliest symptoms: memory impairment
  • Progression: severe impairment in learning new information
  • Early stages: distant memories intact; Advanced stages: distant memories also lost
  • Behavioral changes: depression, irritability, aggression, delusions, hallucinations
  • Ultimately: complete loss of independence

APP and Amyloid Beta (A-beta)

  • Discovered via duplications of chromosome 21 region in affected families
  • This region codes for amyloid precursor protein (APP)
  • APP increased in these families and also in Down syndrome
  • APP normally: regulates synaptic transmission, axonal transport, gene expression, neuronal growth

APP Processing - Two Pathways

  • Beta pathway: APP cleaved by beta-secretase and gamma-secretase to form A-beta peptides
  • Gamma-secretase components: presenilin, Pen-2, nicastrin, aph-1
  • Gamma-secretase determines: A-beta-40 vs A-beta-42 form
  • A-beta-42 is MORE TOXIC and prone to aggregate
  • Alpha pathway: produces p3 peptide (function unknown), prevents A-beta production

A-beta Aggregates

  • A-beta-42 forms: small soluble oligomers + large insoluble filaments (plaques). Both toxic.
  • Small oligomers: can block LTP and disrupt learning/memory
  • Large plaques: damage neurites and interfere with signaling
  • Microglia try to clear aggregates but release inflammatory cytokines and free radicals causing neuronal death

Tau Pathology

  • Tau = microtubule (MT)-associated protein, normally aids axonal transport by stabilizing MTs
  • Normally phosphorylated by kinases causing release from MT (allows transport to proceed)
  • Key kinases: Cdk5 (cyclin-dependent kinase 5) and GSK3-beta (glycogen synthase kinase 3-beta)
  • Hyperphosphorylation of tau leads to neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs)
  • A-beta can: stimulate tau aggregation AND activate kinases that phosphorylate tau
  • NFTs are highly correlated with degree of neuronal loss and cognitive impairment

ApoE

  • Apolipoprotein E (ApoE) - normally involved in cholesterol transport, uptake, redistribution
  • ApoE4 = risk factor for AD (increases A-beta fibrils, A-beta production, can phosphorylate tau)
  • ApoE2 = protective (helps clear A-beta aggregates)

Treatments: Cholinergic Approach

  • Hypothesis: cholinergic neurons in nucleus basalis (memory processing) are significantly affected
  • Early attempt: increase ACh via lecithin intake - NOT effective
  • AChE inhibitors used clinically:
  • Tacrine (Cognex), donepezil (Aricept), galantamine (Razadyne), rivastigmine (Exelon)
  • These are effective for symptoms only, NOT disease modification
  • In clinical trials: M1 mAChR agonists, alpha-7 and alpha-4-beta-2 nAChR agonists, M2 mAChR autoreceptor antagonists

Disease-Altering Strategies

  • Cyclohexanehexols & tramiprosate (Alzhemed) - block A-beta aggregation
  • A-beta vaccine - increase clearance; first trial had encephalitis but showed improvement
  • Beta-secretase (BACE) inhibitors - promising but may affect myelin thickness
  • Gamma-secretase inhibitors - problematic: also cleaves Notch (cell differentiation) causing side effects
  • R-flurbiprofen (NSAID) - alters gamma-secretase cleavage site: more A-beta-40, less A-beta-42
  • Increase alpha-secretase activity - produces more p3, less A-beta
  • Endogenous A-beta peptidases: neprilysin and insulin-degrading enzyme
  • Kinase inhibitors for Cdk5 and GSK3-beta - reduce tau hyperphosphorylation
